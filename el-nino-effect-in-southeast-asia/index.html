El Nino Effect in Southeast Asia: An Overview

El Nino effect is a weather phenomenon that occurs irregularly every few years in the Pacific Ocean. It is characterized by warm ocean currents that disrupt normal weather patterns, leading to extreme weather events in different parts of the world. In Southeast Asia, El Nino can result in prolonged droughts, heatwaves, reduced rainfall, and increased temperatures.

During El Nino, warm ocean currents displace the colder oceanic waters, resulting in a decrease in the upwelling of cold, nutrient-rich waters. This leads to a reduction in the productivity of marine ecosystems, affecting fisheries and the livelihoods of coastal communities. Additionally, the reduced rainfall during El Nino can result in water scarcity, affecting agriculture and water supply for both rural and urban areas.
